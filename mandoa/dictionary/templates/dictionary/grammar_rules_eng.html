Nouns
Nouns in Mando'a have no gender. If necessary, the genus may be determined by context.
In very rare cases, when the gender still needs to be clarified, it is possible to use adjectives jag&x (" male ") or dal&x (" female ").

Possessive
The formation of the possessive in Mando'a mostly depends on the context. The easiest way is to use the preposition be or possessive prefixes be'. The difference between them is the same as between the phrases "Saber Boba" and "Bobina saber", i.e. no.
Example :

Sometimes possessive nouns are attached to an object.
Example :

A rare and obsolete form is the use of possessive words as suffixes 'be or 'b.
Example :

In the infinitive, verbs in Mando'a have the following endings:
- if the root word ends with a vowel, then the verb formed from the word ends with -r;
- if the root word ends in a consonant, then a vowel is added at the end of the verb and -r.

Verb conjugation
In Mando'a, the verb has only two conjugations.
The indefinite is expressed by the infinitive and corresponds to the dictionary form of the word.
The personal form is formed by shortening the sound -r in the verb ending.
The personal form does not change according to times, persons, genders and numbers.
An exception is a small group of archaic verbs.

Verb forms k;&r (to be)
After the language reform carried out by Or'k Rask ( or'ka rask), a Tong who lived in time Mandalore the Ultimate, there are almost no exceptions in verb conjugation in Mando'a.
When other races began to join the ranks of the Mandalorians, and the Mando'a began to speak not only tongs, mastering all the intricacies of the ancient language was too difficult for many of them.
Reform was required, and a number of simplifications and unifications were adopted in the new Mando'a.
But some exceptions turned out to be too tenacious, and the reform failed to overcome them.
The most important exception is the verb k;&r - to be.

The verb k;&r is most often omitted in speech and is used only as a qualifying word.
The meaning is usually clear from the context or, if necessary, can be conveyed via word order.
Basic verb tenses
The present tense of the verb is the most common, the most universal and the most dominant in the language.
If the time of the event can be specified in other words of the sentence or understood from the context,
then the present tense will be used to describe the action.
The present tense is expressed in the personal form of the verb and does not have any additional prefixes.

The past tense is formed by adding a prefix to the personal form of the verb r]' for roots beginning with a consonant,
and prefixes r' for roots that start with a vowel.

To form a verb in the future tense, a prefix is added to the verb in the personal form ven'

Passive
Mando'a is an active language. The Mandalorian is more likely to construct a sentence using direct word order, from subject to object.
But there are times when passive voice is indispensable. Such phrases are used much less often, and or when it is necessary to emphasize in a special way precisely the passive nature of the action, or when the subject is clear from the context and is omitted from the sentence.

The passive voice is built from the object to the subject and a particle is placed in front of the subject de.
In modern Mando'a, the particle de is a preposition, but in archaic texts this particle acts as a prefix and is included in the word.

The very impact on the object can be expressed in two equivalent and equally used ways.

Simple predicate
A simple predicate is expressed by a verb and describes the action which the subject performs on the object.

Negation is placed directly to the verb.

Example:
ni har& I'm tired
ni nq'har& I am not tired
Compound predicate
A compound predicate consists of two verbs:
the main verb, standing in a personal form, and a clarifying verb in the infinitive.

Negation can be put both to the main verb and to the clarifying one.

Between the main and the qualifying verb there can be an adjective in a comparative form,
which indicates a special characteristic of the qualifying verb.

Example:

ni nq'kopAn&epar I do not want to eat
ni kopAn& n'epar al n]hor I don't want to eat, but sleep
ni kopAn& n]hor or&'x&a me epar I want to sleep more than eat
